When do we introduce best statistical practices to undergraduate biology majors? Part 1. Bar plot alternatives

March 11, 2018 in teaching

Within the cell biology literature, but not so much other biology literatures, there have been several editorials and short papers encouraging researchers to use alternatives to bar plots to commuicate experimental results (here, here, here, and here). I suggest a third, a forest plot, or even better a Harrel plot (Hplot), which combines a forest plot with a dot/box plot (xxx).

I could use this post to show how to make the different plots, or to discuss the pros and cons of each plot type, but the focus here is really in addressing the major impediment to any largescale implementation of bar plot alternatives: We first train our undergraduate biology majors using bar plots in Microsoft Excel/Google Sheets, often in the first year Introductory Biology labs. And, humans, being humans, mostly continue using what they were first taught. The major reason we continue using what we were first taught is because there is no “corrective” teaching in upper level classes, or during graduate training. And the major reason there is no corrective teaching is because most researchers are unaware that there is something to correct, or best practices – after all, bar plots are ubiquitous. And the major reason bar plots are ubiquitous is because Excel. There are few resources that easily allow researchers to generate the alternative dot plots or box plots or forest plots or Hplots.

I don’t think there should be correctives. Instead, I think we should start undergraduate training with best practices from the beginning – in the Introductory Biology labs. But we can’t use Excel/Sheets for this. And we aren’t (realistically) going to teach intro bio student R scripting.

These issues have oozed through my thoughts for several years but only really started to crystalize over the last few months. So, Thanksgiving day morning, I decided to teach myself how to make a Shiny App, so that I could create a web-based tool for undergraduate teaching or for researchers who were resistant to scripting in R (or Python or whatever). By the end of the day, I had created Splot, which created simple dot plots.

But what bothered me most about bar plots was not the issues listed in the critical editorials/papers but the fact that bar plots only indirectly communicated what we often want to communicate in our results – the effect size and its error. And dot plots with superimposed means and error bars or confidence intervals aren’t any better in this regard. Finally, my “how extremely stupid not to have thought of that” moment came when I saw this figure from Frank Harrell’s Principals of Graph Construction

Figure from Harrell

This is a pretty good plot. It contains means and confidence intervals of the two treatment levels and the point estimate of the difference in means with its confidence interval. I showed this to several colleagues and grad students and they didn’t immediately notice that the plot superimposes two different components of the analysis – the lower part of the plot is the familiar mean-and-error plot but with a dot for the mean instead of a bar and the upper part of the plot is effectively a forest plot, which is a common way to show multiple outcomes or groups in a meta-analysis in the medical literature.

In the post, Matthew Dalby smartly used the available data to reconstruct the final weights of the two groups. He showed these final weights were nearly the same, which is not good evidence for a treatment effect, given that the treatment was randomized among groups.

What is an R doodle?

 Mar 3, 2018
An R doodle is a short script to check intuition or understanding. Almost always, this involves generating fake data. I might create an R doodle when I’m reviewing a manuscript or reading a published paper and I want to check if their statistical analysis is doing what the authors think it is doing. Or maybe I create it to help me figure out what the authors are doing. Or I might be teaching some method and I create an R doodle to help me understand how the method behaves given different input (fake) data sets.
